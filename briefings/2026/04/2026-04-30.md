Today’s in-scope addition is **On Halting vs Converging in Recurrent Graph Neural Networks**. The paper is a theory-oriented analysis of recurrent graph neural networks, where message passing is iterated until a convergence or halting condition is met.

It fits the collection because the computation reuses the learned graph update/message-passing operator repeatedly within one prediction process. The main value is not a new large-scale language-model result, but a clean comparison of stopping semantics: global convergence, output-level convergence, and per-vertex halting.

The key reader takeaway is that halting and convergence are not just engineering choices. They change how recurrent computation synchronizes across nodes, and the paper’s traffic-light construction shows how locally halting vertices can still coordinate enough to recover expressiveness guarantees.

The nearby recursive multi-agent and multimodal diffusion-latent papers are worth watching, but they stay outside the main collection for now because their recursion is framed at the system or diffusion-process level rather than as a reused learned layer/block inside a single forward computation.
