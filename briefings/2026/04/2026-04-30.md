Today’s in-scope additions are **On Halting vs Converging in Recurrent Graph Neural Networks** and **Associative-State Universal Transformers: Sparse Retrieval Meets Structured Recurrence**.

The recurrent-GNN paper is a theory-oriented analysis of message-passing computation that is iterated until a convergence or halting condition is met. It fits the collection because the learned graph update/message-passing operator is reused repeatedly within one prediction process. The main value is a clean comparison of stopping semantics: global convergence, output-level convergence, and per-vertex halting.

The Universal Transformer paper introduces **UniMatrix**, a structured recurrent-state language-model family that reuses a shared recurrent block across depth. The paper is useful for loop-model readers because it pairs the architectural reuse story with an honest retrieval result: compact recurrent state alone stays near chance on associative recall, while the SparsePointer variant reaches strong recall by adding sparse slot routing and direct pointer-logit fusion.

The key reader takeaway is that recurrence is not automatically enough. In graph models, the stopping rule changes expressiveness and synchronization. In recurrent-depth language models, compressed recurrent state can be parameter-efficient on language modeling, but exact long-range lookup still appears to require explicit retrieval capacity and output routing.

The nearby recursive multi-agent and multimodal diffusion-latent papers are worth watching, but they stay outside the main collection for now because their recursion is framed at the system or diffusion-process level rather than as a reused learned layer/block inside a single forward computation.
